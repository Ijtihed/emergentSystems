Reaction-Diffusion info

Origin

Alan Turing proposed reaction-diffusion as the mechanism behind biological pattern formation in his 1952 paper "The Chemical Basis of Morphogenesis" — his final major work. The Gray-Scott model shown here was formulated by Peter Gray and Stephen Scott in 1983 as a minimal two-chemical system that produces a stunning variety of patterns.

Recent work (2024-2025) shows that on curved surfaces, these patterns become propagating, oscillatory, or chaotic — which is what the deformation brush simulates here.

How It Works

Two virtual chemicals, U and V, diffuse across a surface at different rates and react: U is consumed to produce V, while V naturally decays. Because V diffuses slower than U, local concentrations of V can sustain themselves by consuming surrounding U faster than it arrives — creating stable spots, stripes, and waves from a uniform initial state. The feed rate (f) controls how quickly U is replenished; the kill rate (k) controls how quickly V decays.

Applications

Explains how leopard spots, zebrafish stripes, coral branching, and fingerprint ridges form in nature. Used in procedural texture generation for games and film, material science for self-assembling nanostructures, and computational morphogenesis research.

How To Use

Drag on the canvas to deform the surface — this warps local diffusion rates, causing patterns to distort, split, and go chaotic. Shift-click to seed new chemical V. Right-click to inhibit (erase patterns). Choose a preset for different pattern types. Hit random for a fresh configuration with random seeds and deformations.
