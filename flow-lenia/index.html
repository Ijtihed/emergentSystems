Flow-Lenia info

Origin

Lenia was created by Bert Wang-Chak Chan in 2018 as a continuous generalization of Conway's Game of Life — replacing binary states with smooth values and discrete neighborhoods with continuous kernels. Flow-Lenia, by Erwan Plantec et al. (June 2025), extends this with mass conservation, enabling multiple coexisting "species" that compete and evolve.

Plantec et al., "Flow-Lenia: Towards open-ended evolution in cellular automata" — arXiv:2506.08569, 2025. No public interactive web demo exists for Flow-Lenia.

How It Works

Each cell holds a continuous value between 0 and 1. A smooth ring-shaped kernel defines each species' neighborhood — like a donut of influence. The kernel is convolved with the grid to compute a "potential" at each cell, then a Gaussian growth function determines whether cells grow or decay. Three species (alpha, beta, gamma) each have different kernel shapes and growth parameters, naturally competing for space while total mass is approximately conserved.

Applications

Artificial life research and open-ended evolution — studying how complexity arises without explicit design. Understanding biological ecosystems and species competition. Generative art producing organic, lifelike animations. The mass-conservation constraint mirrors real physics and enables persistent, self-sustaining entities.

How To Use

Select a species from the dropdown and click to place organisms. Scroll to change brush size. Watch species interact, compete for space, and form dynamic structures. Adjust dt to speed up or slow down time. Hit random to scatter new organisms randomly. The status bar shows the total mass of each species.
